History of digital publishing

Despite claims that Publishers have lagged behind other creative industries there has been a large amount of experimentation with digital formats.

Unfortunately much of this was a negative experience...

Timeline of Digital Publishing innovation

1949

Ángela Ruiz Robles patents her “Mechanical Encylcopedia”

1960's

Andries van Dam thought to coin the term “electronic book” through FRESS project at Brown.

1971

Project Gutenburg launched to digitise texts, focused on out of copyright works

Timeline of Digital Publishing innovation

Sony launch the Data Discman a CD powered eBook reader.

eRocket LED eReader launched, e-Ink technology developed

2004-06

Where are we now?

No TCM data?

Unfortunately there is not a TCM Bookscan service which aggregates electronic sales – the closest is volume eBook sales given to Nielsen but these do not include pricing information.

eBook data distributed is based on volume not value which makes it difficult to assess the success of competing digital products.

Where are we now?

Peaking or Plateauing?

Despite constant commentary on the supposed decline of eBook sales the data compiled by Nieslen tells a different story.

In 2014 nearly one in three of all books sold in the UK were eBook formats*, attributed as 47% of Fiction sales, 20% of Non-Fiction and 19% of Childrens’/YA Fiction.

*Source: Nielsen, Books and the Consumer, February 2015

Where are we now?

Peaking or Plateauing?

Although device sales have begun to decline due to market saturation, eBook sales themselves show no signs of “falling away” as some have predicted.

However due to the dominance of certain retailers it is a tough time to launch digital start-ups without relying on existing retailers to support them.

Where is the profit?

There are many potential routes to profitability to publishers but most come with complications.

From increasing efficiencies to maximise product margin to creating new publishing programmes or product lines.

Doing digital right isn’t cheap, easy or quick...

So what should we do now?

Experiment... differently

Experiment... differently

In order to increase the profitability of digital in all the companies across the Bonnier Publishing family we must continue to be bold establishing new revenue models and consumer bases.

eFirst Publishing

Concept:

eFirst commissioning has been growing increasingly prominent, it allows publishers to acquire aggressively into niche communities and zeitgeist trends.

eFirst Publishing

Case study:

By signing debut or established bestselling authors to write new content published as e-first publishers experiment with genres e.g. Chris Ryan: Extreme series.

eFirst Publishing

Pros:

Faster ability to react to industry trends and release similar titles.

eFirst Publishing

Cons:

Discoverability concerns.

Needs to be separate from mainstream publishing in order to maintain and manage author/agent expectations.

Bonnier eReading library

Concept:

Invest in a cloud based library service selling direct to consumers and compatible with mobile and tablet devices through an app.

Bonnier eReading library

Case study:

HarperCollins Reader app – cloud based with direct to consumer ebook sales.

Bonnier eReading library

Pros:

We control every aspect of the consumer journey to our eBooks, most crucially the retailer share of purchases

Bonnier eReading library

Cons:

Extremely high initial costs.

Consumers already have established buying habits.

Content bundling

Concept:

Title/series bundling as per physical boxed sets

Physical and digital book together

The ‘humble bundle’ model of packaging content together.

Content bundling

Case study:

SoIaF series - HC have been very successful repackaging titles from the Game of Thrones/Song of Ice and Fire series by George R.R. Martin at various price points matching titles against the HBO tv adaptation.

Content bundling

Pros:

  • Enables us to reach new audiences – such as reluctant readers and bargain hunters.
  • Creates flexibility for promoting backlist or short-form content.
  • Could provide insight into consumer attitudes towards various price points.

    • Content bundling

    Cons:

    • Replication of work involved in generating multiple eBook editions.
    • Risk of confusing some consumers.

    Subscription Models

    Concept:

    People keep asking when will there be “Netflix” or “Spotify” moment for books – subscription models overtaking standard purchases.

    We should explore this proactively to stay ahead of our consumers.

    Subscription Models

    Case study:

    A number of publishers – Blink and HKB included allowing services like Blloon to sell ebooks (transaction is prompted after 10% of title is read)

    Subscription Models

    Pros:

    Opportunity to reach a new audience as it develops.

    Potential insights into which titles( ten percent samples are most likely to lead to a sale.

    Subscription Models

    Cons:

    Small customer bases at the moment.

    An unconvincing consumer message?

    Partnership programmes

    Concept:

    By working closely with partners unique distribution, licensing and technical opportunities exist which can inform our publishing strategies and increase profit.

    Partnership programmes

    Case study:

    PRH have recently partnered with JellyBooks to gather real time data on reader activity in eBooks.

    Partnership programmes

    Pros:

    Strengthened relationships with partners.
    Increased awareness of future opportunities, and greater scope to become involved early on.

    Partnership programmes

    Cons:

    Partners can have unrealistic unexpectations

    Requires full commitment of resources in order to create commercial successes.

    Audio downloads

    Concept:

    Working across the group to agree an audio programme and partnership with a recording studio we could rapidly release a large number of audio titles.

    Audio downloads

    Case study:

    Hodder & Stoughton Ltd grew their audio download list by over 200% from 2010–2012 changing its significance within the business.

    Audio downloads

    Pros:

    Growing UK market, with an even larger equivalent in the US.
    In some cases we may have recordings we can reuse. Positions Bonnier Publishing as a full service publisher.

    Audio downloads

    Cons:

    Extremely high origination costs - actors, studio, engineers expensive.

    Due to the almost total dominance of one retailer our trading terms would be comparatively weak.

    Responsive pricing

    Concept:

    By changing our systems to allow for live pricing changes we can react quickly to news or in sync with key PR and events.

    Responsive pricing

    Case study:

    We know from the fantastic sales of We Were Liars last year the importance of price promotions.

    When delicately managed the pricing of eBooks have been manually amended to decrease (sales/exposure driver) or increase (better margin on each unit sale) have proven to be crucial to maximising revenue.

    Responsive pricing

    Pros:

    Enables use to maximise the potential sales uplift of PR, events and film or TV tie-in adaptations.

    Responsive pricing

    Cons:

    High amount of in house resource to constantly monitor and adjust pricing whenever necessary.

    Requires direct control of retailer accounts rather than through a service provider which has its own cost and resource implications.
