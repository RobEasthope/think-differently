History of digital publishing

Despite claims that Publishers have lagged behind other creative industries there has been a large amount of experimentation with digital formats.

Unfortunately much of this was a negative experience...

Timeline of Digital Publishing innovation

  • 1949 Ángela Ruiz Robles patents her “Mechanical Encylcopedia”
  • 1960s Andries van Dam thought to coin the term “electronic book” through FRESS project at Brown.
  • 1971 Project Gutenburg launched to digitise texts, focused on out of copyright works
  • 1992 Sony launch the Data Discman a CD powered eBook reader.
  • 1998 eRocket LED eReader launched, e-Ink technology developed

Timeline of Digital Publishing innovation

  • 2004-6 Sony launch the Sony Libré in 2004 and the Sony eReader in 2006
  • 2007 Amazon release the Kindle in the US, Apple launch the iPhone
  • 2009 Amazon release the Kindle internationally
  • 2010 Apple release the first gen iPad
  • 2011 Devices multiply, Amazon release Kindle Fire
  • 17.9 million people in the U.K. own a tablet – over ¼ of the population

Where are we now?

No TCM data?

Unfortunately there is not a TCM Bookscan service which aggregates electronic sales – the closest is volume eBook sales given to Nielsen but these do not include pricing information.

eBook data distributed is based on volume not value which makes it difficult to assess the success of competing digital products.

Where are we now?

Peaking or Plateauing?

Despite constant commentary on the supposed decline of eBook sales the data compiled by Nieslen tells a different story.

In 2014 nearly one in three of all books sold in the UK were eBook formats*, attributed as 47% of Fiction sales, 20% of Non-Fiction and 19% of Childrens’/YA Fiction.

*source Nielsen, Books and the Consumer, February 2015

Where are we now?

Peaking or Plateauing?

Although device sales have begun to decline due to market saturation, eBook sales themselves show no signs of “falling away” as some have predicted.

However due to the dominance of certain retailers it is a tough time to launch digital start-ups without relying on existing retailers to support them.
